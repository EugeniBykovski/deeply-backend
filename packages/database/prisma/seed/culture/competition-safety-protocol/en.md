# Safety in Competition Freediving: How It Actually Works

**TL;DR:** Competitive freediving has a detailed, layered safety system built around each individual dive. Understanding how it works — who is where, what triggers a rescue, and what the risks actually are — is essential context for anyone watching or participating in sanctioned competition.

## The primary risk: shallow water blackout

The safety architecture of competitive freediving is designed around one primary event: loss of consciousness on or near the surface after a breath-hold dive.

This is called shallow water blackout (SWB). It occurs not at depth, but during the final ascent or immediately upon surfacing. The mechanism is physiological: as the diver ascends from depth, the increasing ambient pressure decreases, which causes the partial pressure of oxygen in the blood to drop sharply. If blood oxygen falls below the threshold for consciousness, the diver loses consciousness — typically without warning and without the ability to signal distress.

The counterintuitive aspect of SWB is its timing. A diver who completes a long dynamic swim or a deep descent may feel capable at the surface — then lose consciousness within seconds of breaking the water. This is why safety systems must be in place before the diver surfaces, not after.

## The safety diver system

In AIDA-sanctioned competition, safety divers are positioned along the dive line at specific depths throughout each competitive dive. Their number and positioning depend on the discipline and target depth:

**For depth disciplines (CWT, CNF, FIM, VWT):**
- A safety diver is typically positioned at 10–15 meters — the "danger zone" where oxygen partial pressure drops enough to cause blackout on ascent
- For deeper dives, additional safety divers may be staged at intermediate depths
- The surface safety diver maintains visual contact with the ascending athlete throughout the final phase of every dive

**For pool disciplines (DYN, DNF, STA):**
- Safety divers enter the water alongside the competing athlete and shadow them for the duration of the attempt
- In STA, a safety diver maintains constant proximity to the static athlete
- In DYN/DNF, safety divers pace the athlete from one length to the next, ready to intervene immediately

Safety divers are trained freedivers with specific rescue protocols. They do not intervene unless a defined threshold is crossed — an unconscious or clearly distressed diver. Intervening unnecessarily (before a diver has completed their attempt) results in disqualification, so safety diver judgment is calibrated precisely.

## The surface protocol as a safety mechanism

The Surface Protocol — the mandatory post-dive sequence of mask removal, OK signal, and verbal/nod acknowledgment within 15 seconds — is simultaneously a performance judging criterion and a safety check.

A diver who cannot complete the Surface Protocol is either unconscious or severely compromised. The protocol failure triggers immediate intervention by surface safety personnel. This is by design: the protocol creates a standardized, timed window during which the diver's cognitive and motor function is directly assessed.

A diver who surfaces and begins the protocol but loses consciousness during it (known as a "samba" in freediving — a state of motor dysfunction caused by low oxygen, short of full blackout) is also treated as a rescue event.

## Rescue protocol

When a diver loses consciousness at depth or on the surface, the response follows a predetermined sequence:

1. **Immediate physical support** — the safety diver brings the unconscious diver to the surface and positions the airway above water
2. **Airway stimulation** — loud verbal stimulation ("breathe!"), tapping the face, blowing air across the face — the goal is to trigger the breathing reflex before rescue breathing is needed
3. **Rescue breathing** — if the diver does not resume breathing within seconds, rescue breaths are administered in the water
4. **Medical handoff** — if the diver does not recover within a defined window, they are transferred to on-site medical personnel

The vast majority of freediving blackout events are resolved at step 1 or 2. The diver loses consciousness, is supported by a safety diver, the airway opens, and spontaneous breathing resumes within seconds. The diver typically regains consciousness confused but otherwise uninjured.

Full cardiac arrest events are rare in well-managed competitive freediving. The presence of on-site medical personnel with AED equipment is a competition requirement, but their intervention is the exception in a system that is designed to prevent escalation.

## Medical requirements at AIDA events

AIDA competition rules mandate:
- At least one trained medical professional on-site for the duration of competition
- Emergency oxygen on the competition platform
- AED (automated external defibrillator) on-site
- Clear protocols for emergency escalation to hospital-level care

Venue selection for depth world championships includes consideration of proximity to medical facilities, since hypoxic events at extreme depth, while rare, can produce outcomes that require advanced care.

## What the competitor does

The safety system is not passive from the athlete's side. Competitive freedivers bear specific responsibilities:

**Accurate performance declaration:** Declaring a target depth or time significantly beyond actual training capacity creates risk — the dive takes longer, the athlete is less prepared physiologically, and the safety divers may not be positioned correctly if the dive deviates from the declared profile.

**Adherence to warm-up protocols:** AIDA competitions assign warm-up windows before competitive attempts. Competitors who skip or abbreviate warm-up reduce their own margin.

**Communication with safety team:** Any unusual physical state — illness, fatigue, pressure injury — is the athlete's responsibility to communicate. A dive attempted in a compromised state under competition pressure is a significant safety risk.

**Knowing when to abort:** A diver who experiences equalization failure at depth, unexpected disorientation, or any equipment issue has the right and the responsibility to abort the dive. A red card is always better than a rescue.

## Why this matters beyond competition

The safety protocols developed for competitive freediving have influenced recreational practice significantly. Modern freediver training courses teach the "never dive alone" principle directly from the logic of the safety diver system — shallow water blackout is unpredictable and silent, and an unconscious diver face-down in the water has a survival window measured in seconds, not minutes.

The protocol for recovery — airway support, stimulation, rescue breathing — is taught in entry-level freediving courses because recreational divers need to be prepared to respond to SWB events in training.

Understanding the competition safety architecture makes the logic of these recreational rules clear. The sport isn't managed by rules because accidents are common. It's managed by rules because the physiology of breath-holding makes the consequences of unmanaged risk severe and fast.

---

*Safety in competitive freediving isn't theater. It's a precisely designed system that allows athletes to attempt genuine physiological limits in conditions where intervention is possible if something goes wrong. The system works when everyone — athletes, safety divers, judges, medics — knows their role before any dive begins.*
