# The Mental Game: Calm as a Physical Strategy

**TL;DR:** In freediving, mental state isn't separate from physical performance — it directly determines it. Anxiety increases oxygen consumption. Calm reduces it. Understanding this mechanism makes mental preparation a first-class training concern, not an optional add-on.

## Why the mental game is actually a physical game

Most sports treat mental skills as psychological — motivation, confidence, focus, resilience. Freediving has these, but it adds something more immediate: your emotional state directly affects your metabolic rate, your heart rate, and your oxygen consumption on every single dive.

A diver who enters the water anxious will consume oxygen faster than a relaxed diver at the same depth. The difference isn't marginal. Anxiety activates the sympathetic nervous system, which increases heart rate, raises peripheral blood pressure, and elevates metabolic demand throughout the body. All of this burns oxygen faster.

The mammalian dive reflex — the physiological response that slows heart rate and redistributes blood when the face enters cold water — is dramatically blunted by sympathetic activation. An anxious diver gets less of the cardiovascular adaptation that makes deep freediving possible.

This means that what feels like a mental issue (nerves before a big dive, spiraling thoughts during a breath-hold, tension when approaching target depth) is also, simultaneously, a physical issue measured in seconds of breath-hold time and meters of capacity.

## What composure actually looks like underwater

Composure isn't emptiness. It's not forcing your mind to go blank or suppressing anxiety by willpower. That approach rarely works and costs its own energy in the effort.

What works is specific direction of attention — not away from the experience, but toward the right aspects of it.

**Breath awareness before descent:** Before entering the water, the most effective preparation is attention to exhalation. Slow, complete exhales activate the parasympathetic nervous system through the vagus nerve. This is why breathing patterns like 4-7-8 (inhale 4 seconds, hold 7 seconds, exhale 8 seconds) produce measurable reductions in heart rate within a few cycles. The long exhale is the mechanism.

**During the breath-hold:** Attention directed at physical sensation — the feeling of the water, the position of the body, the movement of fins — occupies the mind with neutral, present information. This is different from distraction. It's anchoring attention to something real rather than allowing it to spiral into projection ("what if I can't make it", "what if I black out").

**At depth:** The most common experience that interrupts composure at depth is the onset of diaphragm contractions. These feel like strong urgency to breathe, but they are CO₂-driven, not oxygen-driven — oxygen levels are still adequate when contractions begin. Knowing this doesn't eliminate the sensation, but it changes the relationship to it. Contractions become information rather than crisis.

## The relationship between training and mental state

Mental composure during a dive is not purely a temperamental trait — it's a trained response.

The most direct way to build CO₂ tolerance (and the calm that comes from knowing you've been here before) is systematic exposure through training. A diver who has spent months completing repeated CO₂-tolerance tables — deliberate breath-holds in controlled conditions — has experienced the sensation of contractions hundreds of times. The sensation is familiar. Familiar things are less alarming than novel things.

This is the training mechanism: not avoiding discomfort, but accumulating calibrated exposure to it. Each successful breath-hold that extended slightly past the diver's previous comfortable limit recalibrates what feels manageable.

**Visualization** plays a specific role here. Before attempting a new depth or duration, rehearsing the experience mentally — in full, felt detail, not just as an abstract image — prepares the nervous system for the actual sensation. Divers who visualize consistently report that the actual dive feels more familiar, even the first time they attempt it.

## Common mental patterns that interfere with performance

**Depth fixation:** Thinking about the number — "I have to reach 40 meters" — redirects attention to outcome rather than process. At depth, this creates tension. Process cues (equalization, body position, fin stroke) are the right objects of attention. The depth will take care of itself if the process is right.

**Recovery anxiety:** After a challenging dive, the mind can replay the experience and amplify the difficult parts. This makes the next dive seem harder than the last one was. Interrupting this cycle — through deliberate refocusing or simply naming what happened accurately — is a recoverable skill.

**Competition nerves:** The announcement of a performance in competition (declaring target depth or time) creates a public commitment. Many freedivers find competition conditions significantly harder than training conditions, not because of logistics or conditions, but because the stakes feel higher. The physiological consequences of perceived stakes are real: elevated cortisol, elevated heart rate, reduced breath-hold capacity.

Managing competition nerves means treating the dive preparation the same way in competition as in training. The warm-up sequence, the breathing pattern, the visualization — all of it identical. Ritual creates predictability. Predictability creates calm.

## Practical tools

**CO₂ tables:** Structured training sessions that deliberately accumulate CO₂ load across repeated breath-holds. Not comfortable, but directly builds tolerance for the sensation that is hardest to manage.

**Extended exhale breathing:** Before any dive, use controlled breathing with extended exhales to activate the parasympathetic nervous system. 4-7-8, box breathing with longer exhales, or simply counting and extending each exhale — the mechanism is the same.

**Body scan relaxation:** Between dives and during the final pre-dive rest period, scan through the body systematically and release any held tension. Jaw, shoulders, hands, and core are common tension points that most divers don't notice until specifically checked.

**Dive journaling:** Writing a brief record after each training session — including mental state observations, not just performance numbers — builds self-knowledge over time. Patterns emerge: what conditions or thoughts reliably produce better or worse dives.

---

*Calm isn't a mood you're lucky enough to feel. It's a physiological state you can train toward, practice accessing, and improve with deliberate effort. In freediving, it's as trainable as your finstroke — and as consequential.*
