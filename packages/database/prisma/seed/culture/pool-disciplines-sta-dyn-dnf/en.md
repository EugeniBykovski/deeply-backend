# Pool Disciplines: STA, DYN, and DNF

**TL;DR:** Freediving's pool disciplines strip away the depth variable entirely. Static apnea tests how long you can hold your breath without moving. Dynamic apnea tests how far you can travel on one breath. These are pure physiological benchmarks — and the training ground where almost every serious freediver develops their foundation.

## Why pool disciplines matter

It's easy to treat pool training as a stepping stone to depth — something you do before you can afford the travel or conditions for ocean diving. That framing misses why pool disciplines are disciplines in their own right.

In depth freediving, performance is distributed across equalization technique, body position, propulsion efficiency, and breath-hold capacity. Isolating any one of these requires removing the others. The pool removes the equalization variable almost entirely (pressure change is minimal in 5 meters of water), which means you can train pure duration, pure distance, and pure breath-hold physiology with a precision that open-water depth training doesn't allow.

Elite depth freedivers spend significant training time in the pool. Not because it's easier — a competitive static apnea attempt is one of the most physiologically intense experiences in sport — but because it's direct.

## STA — Static Apnea

The diver floats face-down in the water and holds their breath for as long as possible without moving. That's it.

STA is the discipline that most directly measures breath-hold capacity — the combination of CO₂ tolerance, O₂ efficiency, and the mental ability to stay composed through progressive physiological discomfort.

**What actually happens during a STA:** The first two minutes are usually comfortable. Around 2–3 minutes, CO₂ levels rise enough to trigger involuntary breathing contractions — diaphragm spasms that feel like the urge to breathe but are not actually caused by lack of oxygen. Oxygen levels remain adequate for several minutes after contractions begin. The experience of contractions is uncomfortable but not dangerous if the diver is on the surface. Managing this discomfort — staying relaxed, not fighting the spasms, letting them pass — is the central skill of STA.

For beginners, a 2-minute STA is a significant achievement. Intermediate freedivers reach 4–5 minutes. Elite STA competitors exceed 10 minutes. The world record is above 24 minutes.

**Training STA develops:**
- CO₂ tolerance — resistance to the urge to breathe under carbon dioxide buildup
- O₂ efficiency — the ability to use available oxygen economically through relaxation and slowed metabolism
- Mental composure — staying calm and unfocused during progressive discomfort

These qualities transfer directly to dynamic and depth performance.

## DYN — Dynamic Apnea (with fins)

The diver swims horizontally through a pool on one breath for maximum distance. Fins are permitted — typically a monofin or bifins.

DYN combines breath-hold physiology with propulsive efficiency. A 50-meter pool is the standard competition venue; elite divers complete multiple lengths. The current world record exceeds 300 meters.

**What DYN tests that STA doesn't:** Dynamic apnea introduces the energy cost of movement. Moving underwater consumes oxygen faster than lying still. DYN performance requires that the diver's propulsive technique be efficient enough that the oxygen cost of each meter swum doesn't outpace their available reserves.

This creates a training problem: working harder (faster, stronger kicks) depletes oxygen faster, but moving too slowly makes the dive longer and total oxygen cost may increase. Elite DYN swimmers find the efficiency optimum — the movement pattern that maximizes distance per unit of oxygen.

Monofin DYN typically achieves greater distance than bifin DYN at elite levels due to propulsive efficiency, but the movement pattern difference means different athletes excel with different equipment.

**Training DYN develops:**
- Propulsive efficiency at low effort levels
- Movement patterning under hypoxic conditions (the last 30–40% of any long DYN is done in significant oxygen depletion)
- Pacing and energy management across a long breath-hold

## DNF — Dynamic No Fins

Same rules as DYN. No fins.

The diver swims horizontally using a breaststroke pull and frog kick (or a modified dolphin-kick from a streamlined body position). No fins, no sled, no rope — just the body and the water.

DNF is the pool analog of CNF in depth disciplines. The oxygen cost per meter is higher because the propulsive mechanics are less efficient, which means DNF distances are shorter than DYN distances for the same breath-hold duration. Elite DNF competition distances are typically 180–200+ meters.

**What makes DNF technically demanding:** Without fins, the diver is constantly negotiating between body position, propulsive stroke, and relaxation. Any unnecessary tension in the body increases oxygen consumption. The stroke must be minimally disruptive while still generating forward movement — a coordination problem that requires specific practice to solve.

DNF is sometimes dismissed as "DYN but harder" — which is technically accurate but misses the point. Training DNF builds awareness of how much movement is costing in oxygen terms, which makes everything in DYN and CWT feel more efficient by comparison.

## Competition format

Pool competitions typically offer all three disciplines, often across separate sessions. Athletes declare a target performance (time for STA, distance for DYN and DNF), and judging includes a Surface Protocol at the end of the attempt: mask or nose clip cleared, "okay" signal given, judge acknowledged.

Shallow-water blackout is the primary safety concern in pool disciplines. It typically occurs as the diver reaches the surface after a long dynamic attempt, not during the dive itself — the sudden pressure change to atmospheric pressure and the reduction in oxygen partial pressure triggers loss of consciousness without warning. Competition pools run with safety divers in the water throughout each attempt.

## For training

Pool disciplines are accessible in a way that depth training rarely is. A lane in a 25-meter pool is enough to train meaningful static and dynamic apnea. Most serious progress in freediving — regardless of whether the goal is depth — comes from systematic pool work.

The disciplines are also honest. You can't negotiate with a stopwatch. You can't compensate for thin breath-hold capacity with better fins or better conditions. STA, DYN, and DNF show you exactly where you are.
