# Maiorca and Mayol: Two Men and the Depth

**TL;DR:** Before training manuals, before AIDA, before standardized depth categories, there were two men whose personal rivalry quietly built the cultural and competitive foundation that modern freediving stands on.

## Enzo Maiorca

Enzo Maiorca was born in 1931 in Syracuse, Sicily — a port city on the southeastern tip of the island. He grew up near the water and began diving for sport in the 1950s, when prevailing medical opinion held that the human body could not survive below 50 meters on a single breath.

This was not a fringe view at the time. Physiologists warned of lung squeeze, blackout, and cardiac failure. Maiorca proceeded anyway.

Throughout the 1960s and 1970s, he extended what was accepted as physically possible in the No Limits discipline — a category in which divers descend on a weighted sled and ascend using an inflation lift bag, with no restriction on assistance. Each record attempt was a direct confrontation with the accumulated conventional wisdom of medicine and human physiology. He won most of those confrontations.

Maiorca brought a Mediterranean directness to his diving. He trained through repetition and self-experimentation. He did not theorize excessively about what he was doing — he went and did it, adjusted, and went again.

## Jacques Mayol

Jacques Mayol arrived from a very different direction. Born in 1927 in Shanghai to a French family, he grew up across multiple countries and absorbed a range of influences that would shape his approach to the ocean. He studied yoga and Eastern breathing practices seriously — unusual for a competitive athlete of his era — and was deeply fascinated by the behavior of marine mammals, particularly dolphins.

Mayol believed, sincerely and consistently throughout his career, that the connection between humans and the sea was not accidental. He thought the body retained something that the rational mind had forgotten. His pre-dive practice was meditative long before meditation entered athletic training culture.

This philosophical orientation was not just aesthetic. Mayol was among the earliest freedivers to systematically investigate the mammalian dive response and to train with explicit attention to parasympathetic activation — relaxing the heart rate down before a dive rather than simply tolerating the depth. The results were significant.

## The competition

For much of the 1960s and 1970s, Maiorca and Mayol alternated depth records in the No Limits discipline. Each time one broke a record, the other answered. The competition was fierce but not unfriendly — both men understood that they were engaged in something beyond personal achievement.

In 1976, Mayol became the first person to reach 100 meters depth on a single breath in a competition context — a benchmark that had been described by medicine as impossible. He had trained for years specifically toward that number, which he had identified as a meaningful threshold.

Maiorca continued competing into his fifties. The longevity of both careers was itself striking — another argument against the idea that the sport was simply young men pushing recklessly into danger.

## The Big Blue

In 1988, director Luc Besson released *Le Grand Bleu* — known internationally as *The Big Blue* — a film loosely inspired by the Maiorca-Mayol rivalry and the world they inhabited. Though fictionalized, it captured something that formal records and discipline descriptions cannot: the particular draw of freediving, the strange quiet of depth, the sense that something human is met rather than simply tested in that environment.

The film was a cultural phenomenon. It introduced freediving to a generation who had never heard the sport discussed, and it created the audience that would support the growth of competitive freediving through the 1990s and into the modern era.

## What they left behind

Maiorca and Mayol never competed within the formal structures that emerged after their era. They predated AIDA's founding in 1992, predated standardized competition protocols, predated the safety systems now considered basic.

But they demonstrated, against credentialed skepticism, that human beings could dive far deeper than medicine had supposed, and that the approach to doing so mattered as much as the physical capacity. One man proved it through stubborn persistence. The other proved it through systematic stillness.

Modern freediving owes more to their particular disagreement than most athletes who practice the sport today realize.
