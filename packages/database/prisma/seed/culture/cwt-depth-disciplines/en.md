# Depth Disciplines in Freediving: CWT, CNF, FIM, and VWT

**TL;DR:** Freediving's depth disciplines each define a different relationship between the diver and the water column. Understanding what distinguishes them — mechanically and physiologically — is the foundation for training intelligently and appreciating competition.

## The vertical challenge

All depth disciplines share one constraint: one breath, one dive, return to the surface. What varies is how the diver is allowed to move through the water, what equipment they can use, and what assistance, if any, is permitted.

That difference in movement shapes everything: the energy cost of the dive, the equalization demands, the time at depth, and the specific physical and technical preparations required.

## CWT — Constant Weight

Constant Weight is the benchmark depth discipline. The diver wears a fixed weight belt that cannot be adjusted or discarded during the dive, and descends and ascends using only their own propulsion — typically a monofin or bifins.

The name comes from the rule: the weight stays constant. No dropping lead to make the ascent easier. No sled assist for the descent. The diver carries what they chose at the surface all the way down and all the way back.

CWT is physically demanding in both directions. Descent requires active finning through the early phase, then a passive freefall phase as the body becomes negatively buoyant (typically around 10–15 meters, earlier with heavier weighting). Ascent from depth — where the body has been significantly compressed — requires sustained, efficient propulsion all the way back to positive buoyancy, then a final pull to the surface.

**Monofin CWT** produces greater propulsive efficiency per kick cycle due to the large blade area and coordinated body undulation. Elite CWT monofin divers achieve significant depth with markedly fewer propulsive movements than bifin divers.

**CWT Bifins** uses two separate fins. The movement is more familiar to swimmers and feels more natural to many new divers. Energy cost per meter is somewhat higher than monofin, but CWT Bifins is increasingly competitive at the highest levels — and remains the more accessible entry point for recreational freedivers pursuing depth.

## CNF — Constant Weight No Fins

Same rules as CWT. No fins.

The diver descends and ascends using a modified breaststroke pull with the arms and a limited frog-kick or dolphin-kick leg motion. Nothing else.

CNF is the most physiologically expensive depth discipline because the diver must push a larger surface area of water with less efficient body mechanics. Moving through the water column without fins takes more time per unit of depth — and more time underwater means more oxygen consumed. The margin between a successful CNF dive and a hypoxic blackout is narrower than in CWT.

Technically, CNF demands exceptional equalization timing. Without the positioning assistance of a monofin or the kinetic rhythm of bifin kicks, the diver must manage body position, propulsion, and equalization simultaneously with less mechanical scaffolding.

CNF practitioners are a minority within competitive freediving. The discipline commands respect because it strips everything back to body mechanics and breath control. There is no equipment advantage to leverage.

## FIM — Free Immersion

In Free Immersion, the diver uses the vertical dive line directly — pulling down to descend, pulling up to ascend. No fins required, though the weight belt rules are the same as CWT.

FIM looks like the "easiest" depth discipline from the outside because the rope does the propulsion work. In practice, it is one of the most technically demanding disciplines for equalization.

Because the diver controls their own descent speed by how aggressively they pull, and can pause at any point by simply stopping, FIM allows — and requires — precise management of equalization timing. A CWT or CNF diver who reaches a depth where equalization fails must either power through or abort the dive; a FIM diver can stop, work the issue, and continue. This capacity for micro-adjustment makes FIM the preferred training discipline for many divers developing their equalization technique.

FIM is also used as a diagnostic tool. A diver working on a new target depth in CWT will often complete the depth in FIM first — confirming that equalization is reliable at that depth before attempting it under propulsive load.

## VWT — Variable Weight

Variable Weight introduces a weighted sled for the descent. The diver loads the sled to descend passively or with minimal effort, then ascends using fins, rope, or both. The weight on the sled can be changed between dives but cannot be released during the dive.

The descent phase in VWT is physiologically distinct from CWT: because the diver isn't finning, the heart rate drops earlier and more sharply, and the cardiovascular profile of the dive differs significantly. The ascent, however, is unassisted by the sled — the diver carries their full positive buoyancy recovery requirement.

VWT appears less frequently in major competition formats than CWT, CNF, or FIM. It represents a middle ground between assisted and unassisted diving that has lost some of its competitive foothold as equipment limitations in CWT have become the accepted standard.

## How they relate in training

Most serious freedivers train across more than one of these disciplines, using each for specific developmental purposes:

- **FIM** builds equalization range and comfort with depth exposure without the propulsive load of CWT
- **CNF** develops raw breath-hold capacity and body awareness — if you can dive well in CNF, CWT feels more efficient by comparison
- **CWT** is the primary competition discipline for most depth-focused athletes; training efficiency is measured against it
- **VWT** is less commonly used in recreational training but appears in some depth camps for controlled exposure to greater depth without full propulsive demand

The discipline you compete in isn't necessarily the only one worth practicing. The physiology of breath-holding improves across disciplines. The body doesn't care what label is on the dive.

*Each depth discipline is a different question asked to the same body: how far can you go, with exactly this much help? The answers — and what they require — are worth understanding before you specialize.*
