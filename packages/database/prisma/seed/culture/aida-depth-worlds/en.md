# AIDA Depth World Championship: How It Works

**TL;DR:** AIDA Depth World Championships bring elite freedivers to a single deep-water venue to compete across multiple disciplines. What sets this competition apart is not just the depth — it's the protocol, the judging, and the system of accountability built around each individual dive.

## The disciplines

AIDA recognizes several depth disciplines for world championship competition. Each defines exactly how the athlete moves through the water column:

**CWT — Constant Weight**
The most widely recognized competitive depth discipline. The diver descends and ascends under their own power using a monofin or bifins. No sled, no rope pull. One weight belt, worn throughout, that cannot be dropped. This is the discipline most recreational freedivers train toward, and the one with the broadest competitive field.

**CWT Bifins**
A variation of Constant Weight using two separate fins rather than a monofin. The leg movement is more natural and the energy demand is different — many athletes transitioning from swimming find CWT Bifins more accessible than CWT monofin.

**CNF — Constant Weight No Fins**
The same rules as CWT, with no fins permitted. The diver descends and ascends using only body movement — a modified breaststroke pull and limited leg propulsion. CNF is physiologically demanding and technically complex. Moving through the water column without fins requires more time underwater per unit of depth, which means more O₂ consumed. CNF is practiced by a smaller subset of competitive freedivers but commands significant respect within the sport.

**FIM — Free Immersion**
The diver uses a vertical rope to pull themselves down and back up. No fins required. FIM looks assisted from the outside — and technically the rope replaces fin propulsion — but it requires a high degree of equalization precision because the diver can control descent speed and pause at any depth. FIM is commonly used as a training discipline even by athletes whose primary competition discipline is CWT or CNF.

**VWT — Variable Weight**
The diver uses a weighted sled to descend (the weight can be adjusted between dives but not released during the dive), then ascends using fins or rope. Variable Weight is less commonly featured in current world championship schedules than CWT, CNF, or FIM, but remains a sanctioned AIDA discipline.

**NLT — No Limits (not currently sanctioned)**
The most extreme depth discipline: weighted sled descent, lift bag ascent, no restriction on assistance. AIDA ceased sanctioning No Limits for world record attempts in 2012, citing safety concerns at the extreme depths required for competitive performance. It is not part of current world championship formats.

## The competition structure

Athletes announce their target depth — the "announced performance" (AP) — in advance. They receive a fixed time window (usually the full competition day, with each athlete assigned a specific start time) to attempt that depth.

A judge descends to the target depth with the athlete's lanyard tag. After the dive, the athlete must complete a **Surface Protocol** within 15 seconds: remove or clear the mask or nose clip, give an "okay" signal, and respond to the judge verbally or with a nod of acknowledgment.

Failure to complete the Surface Protocol results in a red card. A white card confirms the performance. Judges evaluate the dive and the protocol completion independently.

The Surface Protocol is deceptively hard at the end of a deep dive. The diver has just spent several minutes in hypoxic conditions, has experienced significant pressure changes through the body, and must maintain enough coordination and awareness at the surface to perform a precise sequence within 15 seconds. Many competitive freedivers train this specifically — sometimes doing shallow pool sessions just to practice the recovery-and-protocol sequence.

## Where they compete

AIDA Depth World Championships require deep, accessible water — at least 80–100 meters within a manageable distance from a competition platform. Competition has taken place in locations including the Mediterranean, the Red Sea, and the Bahamas.

The logistical scale is significant: safety divers stationed at multiple depths along the dive line, a surface support team managing timing and protocol, medical personnel on standby, and real-time monitoring of each athlete's dive.

## Why it matters

Competitive freediving looks simple from the outside: one person, one breath, straight down, straight up. In practice, it is among the most demanding individual athletic competitions — combining the physiological requirements of elite endurance sport with the technical precision of individual discipline sports like gymnastics or diving, and the self-regulation demands that most sports don't require at all.

The depth numbers mean little without the context of what producing them requires.
