# Loïc Leferme: What He Left the Sport

**TL;DR:** Loïc Leferme was among the most accomplished freedivers of the early 2000s — a meticulous competitor who set multiple world depth records and built a training approach that went well beyond a single discipline. His story includes both the height of what the sport can achieve and its irreducible risk.

## Who he was

Loïc Leferme was born in 1970 in France. He came to freediving competitively and developed into one of the leading practitioners in the No Limits discipline — where divers descend on a weighted sled and ascend using a lift bag, with no restriction on assistance at depth.

No Limits was, during Leferme's competitive years, the most extreme expression of depth freediving. It required elite breath-hold capacity, precise equalization technique across a very wide pressure range, and the mental composure to manage the particular strangeness of being very deep with no fins and no active propulsion.

Leferme set multiple world depth records in No Limits across his career, placing him within a very small group of athletes who had pushed the discipline to what was then its competitive edge.

## How he trained

Contemporaries described Leferme as a methodical practitioner. He did not confine himself to No Limits — he trained across several freediving disciplines, including static apnea and constant weight, treating each as a tool for developing different physiological and technical qualities.

This cross-disciplinary approach was not universal among elite freedivers of his generation. Some specialists trained only in their primary discipline. Leferme understood that different forms of practice — static holds for pure breath-hold capacity, CWT for equalization and body position, No Limits for the specific physical experience of very deep descent — built the platform for his primary performances.

He brought careful preparation to each record attempt, analyzing conditions, refining equipment, and working systematically through the physiological demands of each new target depth.

## His death

In April 2007, Leferme died during a training dive near Nice, France. He was attempting to break his own depth record in No Limits. He was 36.

The sequence of events was not fully reconstructed. He died at depth. His safety team recovered him.

His death was a significant moment for the freediving community — not because the sport was unfamiliar with fatal accidents, but because of who he was and the level of preparation that had surrounded him. If a diver of his experience and methodical preparation could be lost at depth, the conversation about what was acceptable risk in No Limits competition could not be avoided.

## What followed

The No Limits discipline has not been sanctioned for world record attempts by AIDA since 2012. The reasoning involves multiple factors — logistical complexity, equipment dependence, and safety concerns at the extreme depths required for competitive-level performance. Leferme's death contributed to a broader conversation about these questions that continued for years after.

His name remains part of the sport's record history, and his career remains a reference point for what serious cross-disciplinary training can build.

## For those who train

Leferme's training philosophy translates clearly to recreational and developing freedivers: don't specialize too early, treat every discipline as a development tool, and build the platform before you build the peak.

His record-level performances required years of foundation work across multiple disciplines. The depth was the result, not the starting point.
