# Freediving: From Ancient Practice to Modern Sport

**TL;DR:** Freediving as a human practice is thousands of years old. Freediving as an organized competitive sport is about thirty. The distance between those two facts contains most of what's interesting about how the sport became what it is today.

## Before the sport: the diving cultures

The oldest documented breath-hold diving traditions exist in the coastal cultures of East Asia and the Middle East. The ama divers of Japan and Korea have practiced breath-hold diving for over two thousand years — collecting shellfish, sea urchin, pearls, and seaweed from depths reaching 20–30 meters on a single breath. Ama traditions were largely matrilineal; women were considered better suited to the cold water tolerance and breath-hold capacity the work required.

In the Persian Gulf, pearl diving sustained entire economic ecosystems for centuries. Gulf pearl divers descended to 30+ meters using a weighted stone and a basket, completing hundreds of dives per day during dive seasons. The physiological adaptations this produced — enlarged spleens, elevated hematocrit, breath-hold capacity far exceeding untrained populations — were not understood medically until much later, but they were relied upon practically for generations.

Similar traditions existed in the Aegean (Greek sponge divers), Polynesia (pearl and shell harvesting), and along the coasts of Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia. In each case, the practice was economic before it was athletic — a means of extracting food and materials from a resource that was inaccessible by any other method.

These traditions were not "freediving" in the modern competitive sense. They were work. But they established the empirical knowledge that humans can go deeper on a single breath than most people assume — long before science caught up to explain why.

## The competitive origins: Mediterranean pioneers

The transition from working practice to sport competition happened slowly in the twentieth century, with the most visible early chapter in the Mediterranean.

Raimondo Bucher is credited with the first documented competitive depth record in modern freediving history: a 30-meter breath-hold dive in 1949 off the coast of Capri, Italy, made to settle a bet. Bucher was a military pilot — his claim that his training in breath-hold and pressure tolerance prepared him for the attempt was dismissed by the physiologists who predicted he would die.

He didn't.

Over the following decades, Italian and French divers pushed depth records progressively deeper, often against direct medical opposition. Enzo Maiorca, a Sicilian fisherman-turned-competitor, and Jacques Mayol, a French diver with a meditation-influenced approach, became the defining figures of the era. Their rivalry through the 1960s and 1970s produced a series of records that repeatedly crossed limits the medical establishment had defined as unsurvivable.

The cultural impact of this rivalry — and of Jacques Mayol's philosophical approach to the ocean — was eventually captured in Luc Besson's 1988 film *Le Grand Bleu* (The Big Blue), which introduced freediving to a generation who had never heard of it.

## The founding of AIDA

AIDA — the International Association for Development of Apnea — was founded in 1992 in Nice, France. Its creation formalized what had been a loose collection of national competitions and individual record attempts into a recognized international sport with standardized rules, disciplinary categories, safety protocols, and world championship competition.

The founding disciplines were the depth categories already being contested informally: Constant Weight, Free Immersion, Variable Weight, and No Limits. Pool disciplines — Static Apnea and Dynamic Apnea — were codified and added as formal competitive categories.

AIDA established:
- Standardized depth measurement requirements
- The Surface Protocol (mask removal, OK signal, judge acknowledgment) as the mandatory post-dive procedure
- White card / red card judging system
- Safety standards for competition venues
- World record ratification procedures

The founding also created the infrastructure for national federations, judge certification, and the system of sanctioned events that now runs international calendar of competitive freediving.

## The record progression

The depth records of early competitive freediving are almost incomprehensible from a modern perspective — not because modern divers have gone so much deeper in absolute terms, but because each increment through the 1970s and 1980s crossed a predicted physiological limit.

30 meters was predicted fatal in 1949. It was survived.
50 meters followed. Then 70. Then 100, reached by Jacques Mayol in 1976.

After 100 meters, the psychological barrier shifted from "survival" to "limits of human physiology under pressure." The blood shift — the movement of blood into the thoracic cavity to equalize the pressure-reduced lung volume at depth — had been theorized but not well understood. Mayol and Maiorca's continued survival at depths that should have caused lung squeeze demonstrated that the body's adaptation mechanisms were more robust than laboratory models predicted.

The No Limits record progression continued through the 1990s and into the 2000s, eventually reaching 214 meters (held by Herbert Nitsch, 2007, the same year Loïc Leferme died attempting to break his own record). AIDA ceased sanctioning No Limits for world record attempts in 2012.

CWT records have continued progressing under full AIDA sanctioning. The current men's CWT record is above 130 meters. Women's records have followed similar progression, with the current women's CWT record above 90 meters.

## The modern era

Competitive freediving today looks different from the pioneer era in almost every respect:

**Training has been systematized.** The physiology of breath-holding is well understood. CO₂ and O₂ tolerance training, mammalian dive reflex activation, equalization technique development, and mental preparation have all been developed into teachable methodologies. What Mayol intuited through meditation and experimentation can now be taught in a structured course.

**Safety standards are comprehensive.** AIDA competitions require safety divers at multiple depths, surface rescue protocols, medical personnel, and standardized equipment checks. Blackout at depth — the primary risk in competitive freediving — is managed through layered safety systems that did not exist in the early competitive era.

**The sport has globalized.** Early competitive freediving was almost entirely European — Italian, French, Greek. The current competitive field is genuinely international, with record holders from Austria, New Zealand, Croatia, Russia, the Czech Republic, and many other countries. The world championship field reflects the global growth of instructor programs and recreational freediving infrastructure.

**Recreational freediving has grown substantially.** The largest growth in the sport is not at the competitive level but in recreational and course-based freediving. PADI Freediver, SSI Freediving, and AIDA certification courses are offered worldwide, and the sport has developed a broader non-competitive community that treats depth and breath-hold capacity as personal exploration rather than record pursuit.

## What connects the traditions

The ama diver collecting sea urchin at 20 meters in 1000 CE and the CWT competitor at a world championship in 2024 are separated by culture, context, technology, and purpose. What they share is the fundamental physiological experience: the face hits the water, the heart slows, the blood moves to protect the core organs, and the body enters a state that has no close equivalent in any other human activity.

That experience is old. The sport built around it is new. Both are part of what freediving is.

---

*The depth numbers have changed. The body doing the diving hasn't. Understanding the history makes the sport's numbers meaningful — not just as records, but as evidence of what the human body has always been capable of, long before anyone was measuring.*
